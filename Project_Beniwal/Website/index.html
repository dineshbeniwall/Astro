Although the majority of astrophysics is devoted to studying spiral galaxies, as well as the stars and planets that reside within them, the formation and evolution of spiral arms in galaxies remains a basic puzzle. Major advances in this discipline were made mostly in the 1960s and early 1970s. The development of theory, simulations, and observation are all covered in this web project.

Introduction

Introduction and outline...

The universe's spiral galaxies are among its most exquisite and interesting things. The Galaxy Zoo project estimates that spiral galaxies make up around two-thirds of all large galaxies. Star creation predominately occurs in spiral galaxies and is especially linked to spiral arms. The nature of spiral arms must thus be understood in order to comprehend both.

Spiral galaxies are divided into distinct categories based on the existence of a bar (S and SB for unbarred and barred galaxies, and occasionally SAB for weakly barred galaxies) and the degree of winding (or pitch angle) of the spiral arms. Further classification is letter is on scale, from Sa-Sd or SBa to SBd, where SBd is most open arms and SBa is most tightly coiled. The classification also shows a reduction in the bulge's size and brightness from Sa (or SBa) galaxies to Sd (or SBd), as well as an increase in the amount of gas in the bulge from Sa to Sd galaxies. Generation of spiral structures

Generation of spiral structures

This section will dive deep into the origin of such spirals in galaxies. We will cover density wave theory, tidal interaction, bars, and other interactions.

Density wave theory
?

Initially, astronomers believed that a spiral galaxy's arms were made of material. The mass closer to the galaxy's centre rotates more quickly than the matter at the galaxy's edge, thus if this were the case, the arms would become increasingly tightly wrapped (middle video). After only a few orbits, the arms would merge with the rest of the galaxy. This is known as winding problem. In order to avoide this problem the galxie should rotate as a whole (first video) around its center. This is also not possible as it will violate kepler's law.

The most general and wildly accepted theory. Inspired by Lindblad's idea of kinematic density waves in 1960, Lin & Shu proposed this density wave theory in 1964. Lin & Shu assumed the galactic disc is a 'continuum' with stars or gas. They derived the dispersion relation of the density waves for a rotating disc. They proposed that spiral arms are not made of matter but are areas with higher density compared to the surroundings. This greater density area is generated due to moving stars and gases passing through the spiral arms (last video and simulated results depicts an H-II region galaxy that spins like a real galaxy).

There is a perfect analogy given to understand this. This video shows that even though the speed of each point (analogous to stars) is identical, their orbits are disturbed due to some perturbation. This disturbance creates areas in the plane with a higher number of points.

Dynamical background
?

Before deriving the density wave theory, let us familiarize ourselves with general concepts like orbits and resonances. Due to time and space constraints, covering all the derivations with an explanation is impossible. We will briefly go through them and equip us with the concepts to understand further mentioned equations. We will begin by neglecting the gaseous components and thickness of the galaxy and assuming that gravity is the only force. Additionally, take all the stars as equal mass. Now that Boltzmann equation (without any collisions): $$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}+v\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x}\frac{\partial f}{\partial v}=0 $$ \( f=f(x,v,t)\) is the distribution function, which gives density in phase space, and\( \phi\) is potential. $$ \mu =\int f dv $$ This gives: $$ \nabla^2 \phi = 4 \pi G \mu $$ Which is the relation of density with its source, potential.

Quasi-stationary density waves
?

Science, the content of the arms is not static, i.e., continuously in and out materials; we use two more assumptions. One is linearity, and the second is quasi-stationarity. The second assumption is quite bizarre as it assumes the spiral will remain stationary with respect to a rotating frame with some acceptable angular velocity. These assumptions lend us the following: $$ \phi (r,v,t) =\sum_m\phi_m(r)\times exp(i(\omega_m t-mv)) $$ $$ \mu(r,v,t) =\sum_m\mu_m(r)\times exp(i(\omega_m t-mv)) $$ $$ f (r,v,t) =\sum_m f_m(r)\times exp(i(\omega_m t-mv)) $$ Where \(\phi,mu\) and \(f\) are the perturbation's potential, density, and distribution functions. The index \(m\) gives the symmetry components; in our case of spiral galaxies, it is the measure of the number of arms.

The pattern speed gives the speed of spiral patterns and is given by $$ \Omega_p=\frac{dv}{dt}=\omega_{m,R}/m (RED colour) $$ Here \( \omega_m\) can be complex, its real part gives rotation, and the imaginary part gives growth and decay of components.

The spiral
?

Our usual wave equation with \(A(r)\) amplitude $$ A(r)e^{i(\phi (r)+\omega t-m v )} $$ From above, we have wave number $$ k=d\phi/dr $$ Now, we measure the winding of the spiral by the angle \(i\) between the tangent of the spiral and a circle centered at the center of the galaxy $$ tan(i)=\frac{m}{kr} $$ For tightly wounded spirals, \(i\) is small, and \(k\) is large and the inverse is also true.

Tidal interactions
?

Tidal tails are the result of gravitational interaction between galaxies. When galaxies interact, the stars and gas are often stripped away towards the outer regions of the galaxies. Generally, there are two tidal tails, one leading and one trailing the universe. Holmberg proposed the idea first that tidal interaction can cause spiral arms.

Tidal induced arms
?

Recent advancement in technology allows us to run complex simulations. Recent full N body simulations show that tidal also contributes to the spiral arms. Classification of spiral galaxies (The Hubble tuning fork)Dobbs showed his simulation results in one of his publications. The below-attached figure is a simulated isolated galaxy (i.e., no surrounding gravitational perturbations). The figure snapshot time is 300 Myr. The disk has an armed flocculent spiral structure because the stellar+gaseous disc's gravitational perturbation comes into play.

Sundelius showed in 1987, with the help of simulations, that tidally induced spiral arms are indeed density waves, not material arms. But can we have kinematic density waves responsible for spiral arms? Let's choose an perturbation \(m=2\), and a rotating frame such that $$ \Omega_p(R)=\Omega(R)-1/2 k(R) $$ Till first order approximation. Now, we will have no spiral arms when elliptical orbits are aligned along some major axis. However, If orbits are a litter perturbed as a function of radius, we will have spiral arms in the galaxy. This spiral structure's winding will depend on the offset.

If our spiral arms can be kinematic density waves, then we should also investigate quasi-stationary density waves. There is not enough evidence to claim the quasi-stationary density waves. Research shows that there exists uncertainty in the observation, which forbids establishing the claims.

Time scale of tidally-induced spirals
?

With tidal interaction and bars, m=2 spirals are easier to produce. Hence, to see very common m=2 spirals, the tidal interaction should last for a significant time—spiral patterns induced by tidal interaction last longer than local transient arms but shorter-lived than quasi-stationary spiral arms.

We have two factors related to the time period of tidal-induced arms. Firstly how fast they are winding over time, and secondly, how fast they are decaying. Coming back to the first point, let us rewrite the pitch angle $$ tan(\alpha)=\frac{1}{R}\frac{dR}{d\phi} $$ If \(\phi\) has changed \(\phi_o+\Omega_p(R)t\) in time t, then pitch angle $$ cot(\alpha)=\big|Rt\frac{d\Omega_p}{dR} $$ For material arms, the above expression gives a timescale of around 100 Myr. However, for kinematic tidal arms, in the absence of self-gravity, spiral structures last even longer. Simulations of tidally induced spiral winding and decaying give the timescale of \(\approx 1 Gyr\) (Struck et al., 2011).

Bar driven spirals
?

Bars of the galaxies also play an crucial role into generation of spiral structures as most of the spirals starts form end of the bars. Statistically expressing, around \(70%\) two armed spiral galaxies have bars at the center, where as only \(30%\) spirals galxies exist without central bar. This implies that in order to stary conntected with bars, spirals and bars should have same pattern speed. However, the direct connection between spiral arms and bars is still not understood. Considaring the pattern speed there are three possibalities:
  • The spiral arms and bar have same pattern speed. This intuation directly comes from the fact that most bars and spiral arms are connected. Romero-Gomez and other gave a manifold theory to further explain it. It states that the stars which escaped from the Lagrangian points are the bared spiral structure. This means these stars move along with bar, unlike in quasi-stationary density waves where stars crosses the arms. Athanassoula also crossexamined this theroy using N-body simulations and find realistic agreement between theory and simulation.
  • The spiral arms and bar have different pattern speed but connected via some non-linear interaction. According to Sygnet there is small overlap between corotation (CR) of bar and inner Lindblad resonance (ILR) of density wave. Tight-winding approximation allowes the coupling between CR and ILR which enable the energy and anular momentum transfar between bar and spiral arms. Once again this hypothesis algo gets tested on N body simulaiton by Masset and Tagger.
  • The spiral arms and bar have differnet pattern speed and completly independent.

Numerous observational tests are being conducted, albeit they are not yet definitive, to determine whether galaxies exhibit quasi-stationary density waves, tidally or bar-driven spirals, or dynamic spiral arms caused by instability. Most of the observational tests are concerned with the spiral arms' pattern speed and whether or not the distribution of gas and stars is consistent with the predictions made for a fixed pattern speed. The only model that adopts a constant pattern speed is the quasi-stationary density wave one, whereas the other processes produce arms with radially decsreasing pattern speeds.

Pattern speeds
?

Although it is challenging to explicitly quantify the pattern speed, our estimates are still accurate enough. A quasi-stationary spiral density wave has been postulated for the spiral pattern in the majority of data by assuming that the pattern speed is constant. The position of corotation may be estimated, making it the easiest method for figuring out the pattern speed. Using the continuity equation for gas flow over the spiral arms in relation to the rest frame and integrating across each direction, the (Tremaine & Weinberg, 1984) technique yields an expression of the form.

The Tremaine-Weinberg approach likewise assumes a stable state, meaning that the spiral arm is not changing throughout the duration that gas flows through the arm, and assumes a thin disc, but is in theory not constrained by the geometry of the arm. With the radially changing Tremaine-Weinberg approach, no examples of galaxies with a constant pattern speed have been discovered as of yet. In their outer regions, most galaxies have a pattern speed that is gradually diminishing.

Pitch angle
?

We may also think about how well model and theoretical predictions fit the pitch angles of spiral arms in observable galaxies.

The pitch angles of transient star spiral arms in galaxy models with various shear rates were examined by Grand et al. (2013), who demonstrated that the higher shear rates result in spiral arms that are more tightly coiled. It is also obvious that the spiral arms' pitch angle gets smaller with time. The shear rate of model galaxies is displayed against the pitch angles of simulated spiral arms in next figure. Swing amplification is crucial for the generation of spiral arms because swing-amplified spiral arms reach their maximum amplitudes at a specific pitch angle depending on the shear rate, even though spiral arms wind up by differential rotation. Typical pitch angles depend on the shear rate of disc galaxies.

Stellar cluster ages
?

A test of the underlying dynamics of galaxies may also be performed using star cluster ages.

Dobbs & Pringle described this technique (2010). For a constant pattern speed, the ages of clusters should visibly rise with increasing distance from the spiral arm (in the leading direction). The same trend is anticipated for a bar as well. However, there is no material flow via the spiral arms in the case of dynamic spiral arms because of local instabilities. Since there is no projected age pattern, stars of comparable ages are instead seen along a spiral arm. Below figure is demonstrating what we discussed above.

From the numerical models, the patterns for the dynamic spirals appear most robust, because the peaks in the number of stars corresponding to the arms are significantly higher than the noise (e.g. a factor of ∼ 10). For the case of the stationary density wave, the pattern is potentially more confusing, because the peaks in the number of young stars decrease and broaden away from the arm, so they are less distinct. Furthermore after a relatively short time (10s Myrs), the young stars will catch up with the next spiral arm.

Flocculent structures and Grand design
?

The observed frequency of spirals with a grand design and flocculent spirals must also be explained by any explanation or theories of spiral structure. The abundance of galaxies with m = 2 spiral structures has been one of the justifications for quasi-stationary density waves. The preference for m = 2 spirals is explained by the fact that density waves with m > 3 are less likely to remain stable than those with m = 2. Another hypothesis is that the m = 2 spirals are all bar-driven or tide-induced. Since there aren't many solitary grand design galaxies, it can be challenging to identify examples that cannot be explained by tidal interactions. This is partially because it can be challenging to identify really isolated galaxies and those that haven't recently merged (see e.g. Verley et al. 2007).
